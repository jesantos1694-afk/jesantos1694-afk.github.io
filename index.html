How I Think About Automation
Most automations fail because nobody plans for failure.

Everyone wants to talk about the shiny workflow that saves 20 hours a week. Nobody wants to talk about what happens when it breaks at 2am on a Friday. That gap — between demo-ready and production-grade — is where most automation projects die.

After building systems for 60+ clients across legal, ecommerce, education, marketing, and SaaS operations, I've learned one thing: the automation itself is the easy part. The hard part is data integrity, error recovery, and making sure the system still works six months later when someone changes a field name or an API token expires.

The best automation is the one nobody thinks about because it just works.

That's why I focus on architecture over cleverness. Validation before writes. Dedup checks before creates. Fallback paths on every critical step. Monitoring that tells you something broke before your client does.

AI is making automation more powerful, but it doesn't change the fundamentals. An AI agent that categorizes leads is useless if the data feeding it is full of duplicates. A smart workflow that drafts responses is worthless if the webhook payload mapping drops half the fields. AI amplifies your system quality — good or bad.

My job is to make sure it amplifies something worth amplifying.
